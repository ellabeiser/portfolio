What is Google Dorking?

✏️ Google dorking is a technique that uses advanced search operators and keywords to find hidden or sensitive information indexed by Google that isn't easily found through standard searches. For journalists, it's a way to find public but difficult to find information or to just be more efficient in your search.

✏️ Google’s search engine indexes every page on the web and when you type in a search to Google there might be thousands or millions of results. Google tries to guess what you might be looking for when it orders those results. It does this based on your location, the number of times that page has been linked to, where the key words in your search appear in the document, how recently the site was published, among other things.

Advanced search operators help you get more precise results in your search. For instance, "site:gov filetype:pdf" limits results to PDF files on government domains.

site:[url]
Restricts results to a specific website or domain — e.g. only government portals, or a specific company's filing pages.
filetype:[ext]
Finds a specific file type — PDFs, spreadsheets, CSVs. Useful for hunting audits, data dumps, and official reports.
intitle: / inurl: / intext:
Targets where your keyword appears — in the page title, the URL, or anywhere in the body text of the document.
allintext: / allintitle:
Stricter versions: require all your terms to appear together. Cuts noise when two or more concepts must co-occur.
before: / after:[date]
Filters results by publication date — useful for finding what was published during a specific event or time window.
term1 AROUND(n) term2
Finds documents where two terms appear within n words of each other — surfaces contextual mentions, not just pages that happen to contain both words.
term1 OR term2
Broadens the search to match synonyms or alternative phrasings — e.g. supplier OR vendor OR subcontractor.
* (wildcard)  /  -term (exclude)
* stands in for any unknown word in a phrase. -term strips noise — useful when your keyword is ambiguous (e.g. "apple" as a company vs. a fruit).

✏️ Try and think creatively where the information you are seeking might be stored. What kind of document might the information you are looking for be found in? (an investor report, job postings, shipping manifests, customs databases) What format would it be published in? (pptx, pdf, docx, json) And what sites might you want to search? (Government sites, job sites like LinkedIn, trade databases).
